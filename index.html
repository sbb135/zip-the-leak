THE 8 LEAKS

Why insured patients still face costs proper insurance was supposed to prevent

Insurance Leaks

These are the ways insurance still fails insured patients.

  1. High copaysEven with insurance, many patients must pay large amounts at the pharmacy counter to pick up a prescribed medicine. That turns a covered benefit into a financial obstacle at the moment treatment is supposed to begin.
  2. DeductiblesMany insured patients must spend thousands of dollars out of pocket before their coverage meaningfully starts. So a patient can have insurance on paper and still be left paying unaffordable costs when illness first strikes.
  3. CoinsuranceInstead of paying a fixed amount, patients are charged a percentage of a drug's price. That can leave insured patients with very large bills for the medicines they need most, especially when those medicines are new or specialized.
  4. Rebate-driven pricingPlans and intermediaries may receive rebates and discounts behind the scenes, but patients often do not benefit from those savings at the pharmacy counter. As a result, insured patients can be charged based on a high list price even when the net price is much lower.
  5. Copay accumulatorsPatients may receive financial assistance to help afford a medicine, but some insurance designs refuse to count that assistance toward the patient's deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. The money helps in the moment, but it does not move the patient any closer to real coverage.
  6. Insurance that fails when patients need it mostInsurance is supposed to protect people from the cost of illness. But many patients discover only after a serious diagnosis that their coverage is full of cost-sharing, delays, exclusions, and barriers that leave them exposed when protection matters most.

Innovation Leak

This is where the system weakens the incentive to develop future medicines.

  1. Price controls that weaken innovationNew medicines are funded in a high-risk system where a few successful drugs must cover the cost of many failures. When policy cuts the reward for success too aggressively, investors put less money into risky drug development, and fewer future medicines get financed.

Genericization Leak

This is where society loses the long-term payoff from new medicines.

  1. Delayed genericizationThe system is supposed to work by allowing a temporary branded period and then moving drugs into inexpensive generic competition. When that handoff is delayed, patients and payers keep bearing branded prices longer than they should, and society does not get the full long-term value it was promised.
